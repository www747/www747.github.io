Partitioned Regression and the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Theorem
¶

Theory¶

(Based on Greene textbook)

Suppose that the regression involves two sets of variables, $\mathbf{X}_1$ and $\mathbf{X}_2$. Thus,

$$ \mathbf{y}=\mathbf{X} \boldsymbol{\beta}+\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}=\mathbf{X}_1 \boldsymbol{\beta}_1+\mathbf{X}_2 \boldsymbol{\beta}_2+\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}= \left[\begin{array}{ll} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{X}_2\end{array}\right] \left[\begin{array}{l} \mathbf{\beta}_1 \\ \mathbf{\beta}_2 \end{array}\right] . $$

What is the algebraic solution for $\mathbf{b}_2$?

Remember that: $$ \mathbf{[X'X]}= \left[\begin{array}{ll} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{X}_2\end{array}\right]' \left[\begin{array}{ll} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{X}_2\end{array}\right]= \left[\begin{array}{l} \mathbf{X}_1' \\ \mathbf{X}_2' \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ll} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{X}_2\end{array}\right]= \left[\begin{array}{ll}\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_2 \\ \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_2\end{array}\right] $$

The normal equations then are

$$\left[\begin{array}{ll}\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_2 \\ \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1 & \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_2\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l}\mathbf{b}_1 \\ \mathbf{b}_2\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{l}\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{y} \\ \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{y}\end{array}\right]$$

A solution can be obtained by using the partitioned inverse matrix of (Greene appendix A-74). Alternatively, the two individual equations can be manipulated directly to solve for $\mathbf{b}_2$. We first solve equation (1) for $\mathbf{b}_1$ :

$$ \begin{aligned} &\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1 \mathbf{b}_1+\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_2 \mathbf{b}_2=\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{y}, \\ &\mathbf{b}_1=\left(\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{y}-\left(\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_2 \mathbf{b}_2=\left(\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime}\left(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}_2 \mathbf{b}_2\right) \end{aligned} $$

To prove it, begin from equation (2), which is $$ \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1 \mathbf{b}_1+\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_2 \mathbf{b}_2=\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{y} . $$ Now, insert the result for $\mathbf{b}_1$ into this result. This produces

$$ \mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1\left(\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1\left(\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_2 \mathbf{b}_2+\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_2 \mathbf{b}_2=\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{y} . $$

After collecting terms, the solution is

$$ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{b}_2 &=\left[\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime}\left(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{X}_1\left(\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime}\right) \mathbf{X}_2\right]^{-1}\left[\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime}\left(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{X}_1\left(\mathbf{X}_1^{\prime} \mathbf{X}_1\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_1^{\prime}\right) \mathbf{y}\right] \\ &=\left(\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{M}_1 \mathbf{X}_2\right)^{-1}\left(\mathbf{X}_2^{\prime} \mathbf{M}_1 \mathbf{y}\right) \end{aligned} $$

The $\mathbf{M}_1$ matrix appearing in the parentheses inside each set of parentheses is the "residual maker", in this case defined for a regression on the columns of $\mathbf{X}_1$. Thus, $\mathbf{M}_1 \mathbf{X}_2$ is a matrix of residuals; each column of $\mathbf{M}_1 \mathbf{X}_2$ is a vector of residuals in the regression of the corresponding column of $\mathbf{X}_2$ on the variables in $\mathbf{X}_1$. By exploiting the fact that $\mathbf{M}_1$, like $\mathbf{M}$, is symmetric and idempotent, we can rewrite as

$$ \mathbf{b}_2=\left(\mathbf{X}_2^{*'} \mathbf{X}_2^*\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}_2^* \mathbf{y}^*, $$

where $\mathbf{X}_2^*=\mathbf{M}_1 \mathbf{X}_2$ and $\mathbf{y}^*=\mathbf{M}_1 \mathbf{y}$. This result is fundamental in regression analysis.

X1 <- matrix(c(1,1,
               1,4,
               1,5,
               1,6), byrow = TRUE, 4)

X2 <- matrix(c(2,
               7,
               9,
               8), byrow = TRUE, 4)

# Set up projection matrix
P1 <- X1 %*% solve(t(X1) %*% X1) %*% t(X1)

#Set up residual maker matrix (M = I - P)
M1 <- diag(dim(P1)[1]) - P1


beta.hat.2 <- solve(t(X2) %*% M1 %*% X2) %*% t(X2) %*% M1 %*% Y
beta.hat.2
